Product Influence and the Executive Strobe Light

Should product leaders be empathetic chameleons or uncompromising domain experts? A structured debate on navigating executive buy-in.

26 March 2026 · Based on Jessica Fain's insights on Lenny's Podcast

The Central Question

When pitching product ideas to executives, should a product leader primarily optimize for empathy or expertise?

The Empathetic Chameleon

Adapt to the executive's worldview and incentive structure. Treat executives as your primary user. Map your pitch to their psychological state, preferred formats, and OKRs.

The Domain Expert

Challenge executive constraints with objective market truth. Your job is to be the deepest person in the room — not a facilitator who mirrors leadership's comfort zone.

VS

Both sides agree: you must understand the executive's world before you can change it. The debate is about what you do after you understand.

The Reality

The Strobe Light Calendar

The debate opens with a shared understanding: the executive's cognitive state is the fundamental terrain on which all product influence must operate.

Imagine waking up at 8 a.m. and being immediately thrown into a brutal finance meeting where budgets are being slashed. Then 30 minutes later, a massive HR crisis. Then a legal liability review. Flash, flash, flash — complete context switching every 30 minutes. And then suddenly at 2 p.m., you're dropped into a product review.

The product manager has been prepping for three solid weeks. The executive hasn't thought about this feature for a single second. Their brain is still stuck on the budget cuts from the morning. They might not have even had time to use the restroom.

Both debaters agree this is real. Where they diverge is what to do about it.

Position A

The Case for Empathetic Alignment

The Chameleon Argument

Treat the Executive as Your Primary User

Product managers fundamentally fail when they center their own desires rather than acknowledging the psychological reality of the executive they're pitching. The executive simply does not have the bandwidth to center the PM's problems.

This means utilizing discovery interview techniques on your own leadership. When they offer a reactive opinion, don't fight them — ask "What led you to believe that?" Become a communication chameleon who formats information exactly how that specific executive consumes it best.

Why Psychological Safety Matters

The expert side pushes back: why do executives, the ones with all the power, need psychological safety from a product manager?

The chameleon's answer: because making multi-million dollar decisions in a state of constant context switching is terrifying. If you just walk in and say "I am the domain expert, here is the objective truth, take it or leave it" — they will glaze over, check their phone, and your idea dies.

Adapting to their state of mind is the only reliable methodology to ensure good ideas actually survive the executive gauntlet.

It's not about managing their feelings for the sake of comfort. It's about providing the necessary psychological safety so that your market reality can actually be heard.

Position B

The Case for Domain Expertise

The Expert Argument

Don't Shrink the Vision to Fit Five Minutes

If an executive has a limited, frantic view of the world because of that strobe light calendar, catering to that limitation just reinforces it. You end up shrinking the product vision to fit whatever the CEO can process in five minutes.

True influence doesn't come from matching their preferred reading format. It comes from bringing external objective truths to the table — truths so compelling they force the executive to rewrite their own constraints.

The Power of Specific Customer Reality

When the executive wants to cut a feature to save server costs, you don't format a memo about how to accommodate that. You say:

"I watched three enterprise clients completely fail to complete a transaction yesterday because they couldn't find the export button."

You don't build a great product by managing an executive's feelings. You build it by anchoring them to the reality of the market.

The Big Ask

The chameleon's response to limited resources is: "Given our four engineers, how do we make you happy this quarter?" The expert's response is fundamentally different:

The Expert's Move

Change the Scale of the Conversation

"The thing you're asking for is a 10x idea. It's brilliant, but to do it right, I don't just need to reallocate these four engineers. I need eight more people, I need you for an hour twice a week, and I need direct access to the marketing team."

You have to ask for the impossible to build the extraordinary. Influence means shifting the executive's understanding of the constraints — not just submitting to them.

Flashpoint

The "Stuart + Two More" Debate

This design review methodology becomes the sharpest point of disagreement. When an executive demands a specific (often half-baked) solution, the team builds exactly what was asked for plus two alternative options they actually believe in.

The Chameleon's View

A Safe Arena for Alignment

Presenting options proves you aren't blindly pushing your own agenda and proves you aren't ignoring the executive's input. When you show them you've thought through the permutations, you reduce their anxiety. You're building a safe arena where they can engage with your expertise instead of defending their authority.

The Expert's View

A Toy Steering Wheel for a Toddler

If you already know the two alternatives are better, indulging the bad idea by building it out is pure appeasement. It's like giving a toy steering wheel to a toddler in a car seat — it gives them the illusion of control. But if you let them actually choose the bad "Stuart" option because you put it on the table as valid, you're both crashing.

You aren't building trust. You are abdicating your license to drive the strategy.

The Deeper Tension

The expert argues that spending valuable engineering and design cycles building an idea you know is bad is a waste of resources. The real trust-builder isn't workshopping alternatives — it's the willingness to look at an executive's preferred idea and confidently say: "We tested this, it's not working, and here is exactly why we need to kill it."

The chameleon counters: that's only possible if you've first laid the groundwork of empathy. Without it, killing their idea doesn't look like visionary leadership — it looks like tone-deaf entitlement.

The Economics

Product Funding as Internal VC

The chameleon introduces a frame that redefines the power dynamic: product funding — engineering headcount, marketing spend, design resources — is essentially internal venture capital, granted by the executive team.

To secure that investment, you must explicitly connect your pitch to the OKRs or board pressures the CEO is facing right now. You have to figure out what's keeping the CEO awake at 2 a.m. and make your feature the solution to that specific nightmare.

The "Painting the Cabinets" Example

Slack's product team wanted to focus on craft — fixing rough edges in the user experience because it was the right thing to do. But they didn't demand it as purists.

Alignment in Action

Translating Craft into Market Differentiation

The team framed their desire for craft entirely through the lens of the executive's incentive: the belief that their market differentiation had slipped. They translated "painting the inside of the cabinets" into "reclaiming our competitive moat."

That is influence — taking your desire and mapping it perfectly onto their pressure points.

The Expert's Rebuttal: The Incrementalism Trap

If your only lens for securing internal VC money is asking "what is the board pushing you on right now?" you're implicitly agreeing that the board's current anxiety is the only valid constraint. That limits your vision to pure incrementalism.

You don't change the trajectory of a company by figuring out how to neatly fit your pitch into this quarter's OKRs. You do it by bringing an external market reality so compelling it forces the executive to rewrite the OKRs entirely.
The Future

The AI Paradigm Shift

Both sides agree that AI is fundamentally reshaping the role of the product manager. When an AI agent can spin up a functional prototype in 10 seconds, the friction of creation disappears. The PM transitions entirely into a director of work, managing an army of AI agents.

The Chameleon's AI Thesis

Alignment Becomes the Ultimate 10x Skill

When ideas are cheap and execution is nearly free, the only bottleneck is getting the CEO to say yes. The only differentiator is your ability to understand human executives deeply enough to get them to greenlight the output. PM survival depends entirely on synthesizing AI's output into a narrative the CEO's strobe-light brain can process.

The Expert's AI Thesis

When Everyone's a Chameleon, Nobody Is

If everyone — from the intern to the CFO — can generate a beautifully polished, empathetic pitch deck with a single prompt, being a communication chameleon is no longer a moat. It's just baseline table stakes.

The critical bottleneck becomes rigorous judgment and human taste. The domain expert is the one with the courage to look at an AI-generated idea the executive loves and say: "This lacks soul. This doesn't actually solve the human problem."

We will have so much software we won't know what to do with it. As AI scales execution, the courage to reject strategically empty ideas becomes far more important than the ability to build consensus around them.
Resolution

Where Both Sides Converge

Despite the sharp exchanges, the debate reveals a surprising amount of shared ground.

Point of Agreement

Understanding Is the Non-Negotiable Prerequisite

Both sides agree: you cannot successfully pitch an idea or challenge a constraint without first doing the hard work of discovering the foundational beliefs and pressures driving your executive. You have to know what's causing the strobe light to flash before you can navigate through it.

Point of Agreement

The Role Is Shifting from Execution to Strategic Editing

As execution gets easier, the product manager's role shifts from managing sprints to curating which ideas deserve to exist. The execution is no longer the bottleneck — strategy and alignment around that strategy are.

The Dual Nature of Modern Leadership

The debate's final synthesis captures the real takeaway: it's not a binary choice.

You have to master the ability to be the chameleon who reads the room perfectly, while simultaneously maintaining the steel of a steadfast expert who refuses to lose sight of the market reality. It is not an easy line to walk, but it is the only way great products actually get built.

The Parting Question

If building a product is no longer just about writing code that objectively compiles, but navigating the messy, subjective minds of the people funding it — are you just optimizing the machine, or are you actually managing the humans running it?
