Part One

Influence Is Not Office Politics

When we hear "influence," we picture corporate manipulation — cornering executives on golf courses, hoarding information, flattering egos. But politics and influence operate on entirely different mechanics.

Politics is a system hack designed entirely for personal gain. It functions like a virus: it serves only itself, bypasses the company's immune system, and extracts resources at the host's expense.

Influence is writing a patch for the company's operating system so great ideas can actually run smoothly. It removes friction so that the best objective path forward survives the gauntlet of human decision-making.

An idea that only exists in your head or on your local machine is basically just a hallucination. Influence is the kinetic energy that forces that idea into reality.

The dangerous cognitive bias builders suffer from: assuming their work speaks for itself. Pure domain expertise is static. Knowledge, no matter how profound, is useless without the momentum required to bring other human beings along.

Part Two

The Strobe-Light Executive Mind

Product teams are trained to cultivate boundless empathy for users — hours of interviews, detailed pain-point mapping. But the second they step into a room to pitch an internal executive, that empathy evaporates.

Jessica Fain uses the metaphor of a strobe light to describe the executive's calendar. They don't have the luxury of deep focus. Their day is a cascade of disparate crises: a tense budget meeting, then a VP interview, then a confidential legal consultation — and then, with zero decompression, your product review.

Think of their brain as a web browser with 150 tabs open. The internal cooling fan is whirring like a jet engine. And chaotic loud music is playing from one of those 150 tabs — and they can't figure out which one.

Local Maximum vs. Global Maximum

The presenter optimizes for their local maximum — making this specific project successful. The executive is responsible for the global maximum — the survival and growth of the entire company ecosystem. When they seem distracted or ask unrelated questions, that's not incompetence. That rapid context-switching is the core feature of their job.

Part Three

The 60-Second Rule

You cannot launch into a review of your dataset or a demo of the prototype. You have to manually load context into their working memory in the first 30–60 seconds.

Framework

The 60-Second Context Reset

  1. Why are we here today?
  2. Where did we leave off the last time we discussed this topic?
  3. What is the specific, tangible goal of today's meeting?
  4. Was there anything else you were hoping to cover before we dive in?

That final question surfaces latent anxieties before they can derail your presentation. You're telling their brain it's safe to close the other tabs for the next 20 minutes.

The Hardest Part: The 61st Second

You have to stop talking. If you spend five minutes rambling about project history before stating the goal, you've failed — you've become another source of chaotic noise in their strobe-light day.

Handling Interruptions

If the executive hijacks the agenda at second 15 with an unrelated anxiety, don't force them back to your script. Acknowledge, validate, and park it:

"Infrastructure capacity is a massive priority. We actually have a slide dedicated to server load at the end of this deck. Does it work for you if we cover the user flow first and then dive deep into the technical constraints?"
Part Four

The Pitch as Discovery Interview

If you walk into a pitch hoping leadership just nods and waves you through, you aren't doing product management — you're looking for a pat on the head. Seeking validation is a failure state.

Every pitch should be treated as a discovery interview: strengthening your plan by extracting and integrating the executive's domain expertise.

"It's not my fault, but it is my problem."

— Annie Pearl, CPO at Calendly & Glassdoor

This eliminates the victim mentality. You can't retreat to your desk and complain that leadership is too short-sighted. It is your problem to figure out the translation layer between your granular data and their high-level anxiety.

The Magic Phrase

When an executive suggests something that contradicts your data, do not say "the data proves you're wrong." Instead:

Linguistic Tool

"That's so interesting. What led you to believe that?"

This bypasses the ego entirely. You validate their intellect through genuine curiosity while forcing them to show their work. You'll rarely find they're acting maliciously — you'll discover invisible pressures: a threatening enterprise client, a board mandate on different revenue metrics.

The standoff transforms into a co-creation session. You're no longer fighting — you're sitting on the same side of the table, reconciling your user data with their enterprise pressure.

The Noah Vice Notebook

An early product leader at Slack, Noah Vice maintained a small physical notebook recording things he learned from CEO Stewart Butterfield. Not sycophancy — functional reverse engineering. By mapping the executive's intuition, Noah could scale product principles without needing the CEO in every room and reflect foundational philosophies back when pitching controversial directions.

Part Five

Intelligence Gathering & Incentive Mapping

Before opening your presentation software, you must answer: How does my idea mathematically or logically support the goal this executive is personally responsible for?

Ask Questions with Edge

"What's top of mind for you?" is a corporate trope that generates polished non-answers. Instead:

Better Questions

Pierce the Corporate Veil

  • "What is the board pushing you hardest on right now?"
  • "What are you legitimately terrified of messing up this quarter?"

If you lack direct access, use the executive's orbit: their EA, chief of staff, peer managers who recently got greenlit. Ask: "What is currently keeping the boss awake at 3 a.m.?"

The Theory of Change

Borrowed from the nonprofit sector, this framework bridges the gap between your feature and the company's primary goal through a defensible logical chain:

Example

Push Notification Settings → Enterprise Churn

  1. Redesigned notification settings reduces user fatigue
  2. Reduced fatigue drives higher daily active engagement
  3. Higher engagement improves the enterprise account health score
  4. Better health scores directly impact the churn metric keeping the CEO awake

Case Study: The Customer Love Sprint

At Slack, leadership felt the product had "lost its magic" — they wanted teams to "paint the insides of the cabinets." Fain's team paused all roadmap engineering for two weeks, letting engineers pick their own rough-edge fixes. The result: 65 user experience improvements in two weeks.

The team mapped the local action (bug fixing) directly to the global maximum (brand differentiation through product craft). They didn't ask permission to fix bugs — they designed a contained sprint that soothed the executive's specific anxiety.

Part Six

Packaging: The Minto Pyramid & Permutations

Become a Communication Chameleon

Every leader processes cognitive load differently. Some need a six-page written narrative in silence. Others want an interactive slide deck. Some trust only live dashboard data. Others are moved only by recorded customer videos. Forcing the wrong format creates unnecessary translation friction before they can evaluate your idea.

Do Not Overprove Your Work

When you've spent months on a project, the instinct is to show your math. Resist it. The baseline expectation is that you already did rigorous work — you wouldn't be in the room otherwise. Put the methodology in the appendix. Let them ask for it if they doubt your conclusion.

Never Present Just One Option

A single recommendation triggers the executive's critical instinct: their job is to find flaws. If you only give them Plan A, their brain generates hypothetical alternatives. Three options prove you've surveyed the landscape and channel their critical energy into comparing your curated set.

Case Study

The Rachel Story

Fain's team pitched CPO Rachel with a single bulletproof recommendation — it bombed. Two days later, they presented the exact same recommendation, but included all the alternative paths they'd explored and discarded, with rationale. Rachel immediately greenlit it. Same outcome, different psychological journey — she needed to see the guardrails.

The Minto Pyramid

Developed at McKinsey in the 1950s by Barbara Minto, this framework completely inverts the academic style. Executives despise the grand reveal — they want the spoiler immediately.

Framework

Start at the Top

  1. Top: Your final recommendation (the answer)
  2. Middle: Options considered and core supporting arguments
  3. Base: Granular data and evidence (only if requested)

Stuart + Two More

At Slack, when CEO Stewart Butterfield dictated a specific design change, the team didn't blindly build it. They built his version plus two alternatives the design team believed in. Placing his idea next to professional alternatives forced genuine debate on merit rather than positional authority — elevating the relationship from parent-child to peer-to-peer.

Part Seven

Dismantling the Perception of Risk

Know When to Fold

If resistance is built on deeply held foundational beliefs about the market, drop it. Don't die on every hill. Preserve your political capital for battles where you have a mathematical chance of winning. Pivot to one of your other nine good ideas.

Shrinking the Change

From the book Switch: when resistance is simply fear of the unknown, make the ask so small that "no" becomes unreasonable.

Tactic

Lower the Threshold of Permission

Don't ask for six months and $2M. Ask for a one-week proof of concept or a two-day hackathon to validate a single high-risk assumption. You're not asking them to bet the company — you're asking them to buy a very cheap insurance policy.

Kill Your Own Darlings

The most controversial and most powerful move: voluntarily terminate your own projects when the data says they aren't working. In a culture of "do more with less," hoarding busy work feels safe — but executives eventually notice the lack of impact.

Killing your darling sends an unmistakable signal: I care more about the global maximum of the company than the local maximum of my own ego. Executives rarely fire leaders who optimize resource allocation — they reallocate them to harder, more vital problems.

Handling Executive Impulses

When the CEO reads an article on a flight and demands a blockchain pivot, don't drop everything — but don't flatly reject them either:

"How strongly do you feel about this new direction? Does this blockchain integration trump our previously agreed-upon priorities X, Y, and Z?"

Making the trade-offs visible forces them to contextualize the brainstorm against cold reality. They'll usually wave it off to the backlog.

Part Eight

Influence in the AI Era

Fain calls this the golden age of product management. The hard skills of execution are being rapidly commoditized — AI can write code, transcribe meetings, run A/B tests. If your entire value was managing the Gantt chart or writing clean boilerplate, your leverage is gone.

The new constraints are distribution and trust. With a tsunami of software incoming, human value shifts entirely from doing the work to deciding what work deserves to survive.

If you point a swarm of AI coding agents in the wrong direction, they won't just waste a week. They will build a massive, useless, fully functioning architecture in an afternoon.

We are evolving into directors of work, managing armies of autonomous agents. The critical insight: we have to deploy the exact same frameworks of influence on our AI agents that we use on human executives. Deep context, codified philosophy, explicit success metrics, guardrails — the 60-second rule and incentive alignment, but for a machine.

What AI still lacks: the ability to function as an anthropologist. It can't read the unspoken tension in a boardroom or notice the subtle physical frustration of a user. Those deeply human emotional elements are the soil where truly novel strategy is cultivated.

Part Nine

Authenticity & Empathy as Fuel

Adopting an inauthentic "alpha persona" usually destroys trust. People — and increasingly algorithms — can detect inauthenticity. Fain was explicitly told early in her career to "act more like a man." She discovered that wearing that mask drained her energy and alienated her peers.

Influence is infinitely more potent when it's deeply authentic to your own psychological makeup. Fain leaned into her natural communal instincts to weave intricate networks of trust across organizations.

Two Guiding Mottos

"First the Guests"

When you enter a negotiation, structurally prioritize the comfort, metrics, and anxieties of the other party before your own. When a stressed executive feels you're genuinely putting their needs first, their defensive posture collapses.

"If You Want a Village, Be a Villager"

If you want cross-functional allies to back your ideas, you have to be the one offering help, reviewing documents, and supporting their initiatives first.

Training Empathy Through Windows

Fain divides media into mirrors (reflecting our own experience) and windows (peering into unfamiliar worlds). She deliberately consumes historical fiction as empathy training: Pachinko, Homegoing, A History of Burning, The Overstory. If you can empathize with a 17th-century character, you can train yourself to empathize with a stressed executive sweating over a budget.

Preserve Your RAM

We are physical creatures subject to burnout. Fain finds joy in a $35 towel warmer and uses a household management service (Casa) to reclaim mental bandwidth. Reducing daily-life friction preserves the cognitive energy you need to process the 150 open tabs of the executive you're trying to influence tomorrow morning.
